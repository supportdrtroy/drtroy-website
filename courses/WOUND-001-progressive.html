Phases of Wound Healing

Wound healing occurs through a complex series of overlapping phases that restore tissue integrity.

1. Hemostasis (Minutes to Hours)

  • Vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation
  • Formation of fibrin clot
  • Release of growth factors and cytokines

2. Inflammatory Phase (Days 1-6)

  • Vasodilation and increased permeability
  • Neutrophil infiltration (first 24-48 hours)
  • Macrophage activation (days 2-6)
  • Phagocytosis of bacteria and debris

3. Proliferative Phase (Days 3-21)

  • Angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation)
  • Fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis
  • Granulation tissue formation
  • Epithelialization from wound edges

4. Maturation/Remodeling (Weeks to Years)

  • Collagen remodeling and cross-linking
  • Wound contraction
  • Scar formation and strengthening
Clinical Pearl: Chronic wounds often stall in the inflammatory phase. Identifying and addressing barriers to healing is essential for progression.

Comprehensive Wound Assessment

Systematic wound assessment guides treatment decisions and monitors healing progress.

Wound Assessment Components

  • Location: Anatomical site, pressure points
  • Size: Length x width x depth in centimeters
  • Tunneling/Undermining: Clock method documentation
  • Wound bed: Tissue types and percentages
  • Exudate: Amount, color, consistency, odor
  • Periwound: Skin condition, edema, erythema
  • Pain: Location, intensity, quality

Pressure Injury Staging (NPIAP 2016)

The National Pressure Injury Advisory Panel staging system provides consistent classification.

Stage 1 - Non-Blanchable Erythema

  • Intact skin with non-blanchable redness
  • Usually over bony prominence
  • May be difficult to detect in dark skin tones

Stage 2 - Partial Thickness

  • Partial-thickness loss of dermis
  • Shiny or dry shallow ulcer without slough
  • May present as intact or ruptured blister

Stage 3 - Full Thickness

  • Full-thickness tissue loss
  • Subcutaneous fat may be visible
  • No bone, tendon, or muscle exposed

Stage 4 - Full Thickness with Exposed Structures

  • Full-thickness tissue loss with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle
  • Often includes undermining and tunneling

Unstageable

Full-thickness wound with base covered by slough or eschar, preventing true depth assessment.

Deep Tissue Pressure Injury

Purple or maroon localized area of discolored intact skin or blood-filled blister due to damage of underlying soft tissue.

Important: Never use staging to describe wounds other than pressure injuries (e.g., diabetic foot ulcers, arterial ulcers).

Recognizing Wound Infection

Wound infection occurs when bacterial burden exceeds host immune defenses and impedes healing.

Classic Signs of Infection

  • Erythema: Spreading redness from wound edges
  • Warmth: Localized heat at wound site
  • Edema: Swelling of surrounding tissue
  • Purulent drainage: Yellow, green, or brown exudate
  • Malodor: Foul smell
  • Pain: Increasing or changing character

Biofilm Considerations

Biofilms are communities of bacteria encased in protective extracellular polymeric substance. They are present in most chronic wounds and require aggressive debridement.

Clinical Warning: Signs of systemic infection include fever, elevated WBC, tachycardia, confusion in elderly, and lymphangitis. These require immediate medical attention.

Debridement Techniques

Debridement removes devitalized tissue, reducing bacterial burden and facilitating wound healing.

MethodTypeBest For
AutolyticBiologicalMinimal devitalized tissue, intact immune system
EnzymaticChemicalPatients who cannot tolerate sharp debridement
Sharp/SurgicalMechanicalLarge amounts of necrotic tissue, infection
MechanicalPhysicalSlough removal (wet-to-dry rarely recommended)
Biosurgical (Larvae)BiologicalLarge necrotic wounds, osteomyelitis

Contraindications to Debridement

  • Dry, stable eschar on ischemic heels (do not debride)
  • Uncontrolled bleeding disorders
  • Exposed blood vessels, tendons without coverage
  • Active infection without antibiotic coverage

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

NPWT applies subatmospheric pressure to promote wound healing through multiple mechanisms.

Benefits of NPWT

  • Removes exudate and infectious materials
  • Increases blood flow to wound bed
  • Promotes granulation tissue formation
  • Reduces edema
  • Approximates wound edges

Contraindications

  • Untreated osteomyelitis
  • Malignancy in wound
  • Exposed blood vessels or organs
  • Non-enteric and unexplored fistulas
  • Necrotic tissue with eschar present
