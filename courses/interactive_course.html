Course Information

Course Description

This comprehensive continuing education course provides physical therapy professionals with systematic, evidence-based approaches to musculoskeletal evaluation and treatment across all major body regions. Participants will learn examination techniques, special tests with diagnostic accuracy metrics, differential diagnosis strategies, and evidence-informed interventions for the cervical spine, thoracic spine, lumbar spine, sacroiliac joint, shoulder, elbow, wrist/hand, hip, knee, and ankle/foot.

Target Audience

This course is designed for licensed physical therapists seeking to enhance their musculoskeletal examination skills and clinical decision-making abilities.

Course Objectives

Upon completion of this course, participants will be able to:

  1. Perform comprehensive regional musculoskeletal evaluations using systematic, evidence-based examination approaches
  2. Apply and interpret special tests with knowledge of sensitivity, specificity, likelihood ratios, and clinical utility
  3. Identify red flags and recognize when immediate physician referral is indicated
  4. Develop differential diagnoses based on subjective history and objective examination findings
  5. Design evidence-based, region-specific treatment plans tailored to specific pathologies
  6. Integrate examination findings to formulate clinical diagnoses and prognoses

Module 1: Introduction to Musculoskeletal Evaluation

Learning Objectives:
  • Understand the five-component examination model
  • Recognize the importance of red flag screening
  • Appreciate the relationship between structure and pain

The Examination Model

Effective musculoskeletal examination follows a systematic five-component model that ensures comprehensive assessment while maintaining efficiency. This model has been refined through decades of clinical practice and research.

Component 1: History

The subjective history forms the foundation of your clinical reasoning. Key elements include:

  • Mechanism of injury: Traumatic vs. insidious onset
  • Symptom location: Primary complaints and referred symptoms
  • Onset and duration: Acute, subacute, or chronic presentation
  • Aggravating factors: Activities, positions, or movements that worsen symptoms
  • Relieving factors: What helps and what doesn't
  • Prior episodes: Previous similar injuries or treatments
  • Current functional limitations: Impact on activities of daily living
  • Red flag screening: Signs of serious pathology

Component 2: Observation

Visual assessment provides valuable information before physical contact:

  • Posture: Static and dynamic alignment
  • Gait patterns: Antalgic, Trendelenburg, or other compensatory patterns
  • Swelling: Joint effusion or soft tissue edema
  • Muscle atrophy: Disuse or neurological involvement
  • Scars: Previous surgeries or trauma
  • Willingness to move: Guarding or pain behaviors

Component 3: Movement Testing

Assessment of both active and passive movement provides insight into tissue involvement:

  • Active Range of Motion (AROM): Patient-generated movement
  • Passive Range of Motion (PROM): Therapist-guided movement
  • Quantity of movement: Degrees of motion available
  • Quality of movement: Smoothness, rhythm, and compensatory patterns
  • Painful arcs: Specific ranges where pain occurs
  • End-feel assessment: Tissue resistance at end range

Component 4: Special Tests

Provocative tests help identify specific tissue involvement:

  • Sensitivity: Ability to correctly identify positive cases
  • Specificity: Ability to correctly identify negative cases
  • Likelihood ratios: How much the test changes probability
  • Clinical utility: Practical application in clinical decision-making
Clinical Pearl: Special tests should confirm or rule out hypotheses generated from your history and initial examination. They are not fishing expeditions but targeted investigations based on your clinical reasoning.

Component 5: Palpation

Manual examination of tissues, performed last to avoid sensitization:

  • Anatomical landmarks: Bony prominences and tissue borders
  • Tissue texture: Muscle tension, fascial restrictions
  • Temperature: Local inflammation or circulation
  • Point tenderness: Specific areas of sensitivity

Red Flag Screening

The most critical aspect of any examination is screening for serious pathology requiring immediate medical attention.

Red Flags - Immediate Physician Referral Required:
  • Cancer signs: Unexplained weight loss, night pain, age >50 with new back pain
  • Infection signs: Fever, chills, recent bacterial infection
  • Fracture signs: Significant trauma, age >70, prolonged steroid use
  • Cauda equina syndrome: Bowel/bladder dysfunction, saddle numbness, bilateral neurological symptoms
  • Vascular compromise: Absent pulses, compartment syndrome signs

Structure vs. Pain

One of the most important concepts in modern musculoskeletal practice is understanding that structural findings do not always correlate with pain and dysfunction.

Evidence-Based Insight: Multiple studies show that asymptomatic individuals frequently have "abnormal" imaging findings. For example, 30% of 20-year-olds have disc degeneration on MRI despite no back pain. Clinical correlation is essential.

Key principles include:

  • Clinical correlation: Symptoms must match examination findings
  • Movement-based assessment: Function often more relevant than structure
  • Patient-centered approach: Address functional limitations, not just imaging findings
  • Evidence-informed treatment: Base interventions on clinical presentation, not solely on diagnostic imaging

This systematic approach ensures thorough evaluation while maintaining clinical efficiency. In the following modules, we will apply this framework to each major body region.

  • Recognize red flags requiring immediate referral
  • Apply evidence-based special tests for cervical pathology

Cervical Anatomy Review

The cervical spine consists of seven vertebrae with unique anatomical features that allow for extensive mobility while protecting vital neurovascular structures.

Key Anatomical Landmarks

  • C1 (Atlas): No vertebral body, supports the skull
  • C2 (Axis): Contains the odontoid process
  • C3-C6: Typical cervical vertebrae with bifid spinous processes
  • C7 (Vertebra prominens): Prominent spinous process, easily palpable
Figure 5: Cervical Spine Anatomy
The cervical spine consists of seven vertebrae (C1-C7) with unique anatomical features that allow for extensive mobility while protecting vital neurovascular structures.

Cervical Examination

Red Flags - Immediate Medical Referral

  • Vertebrobasilar insufficiency: Drop attacks, dizziness with head movement, nausea
  • Upper motor neuron signs: Hyperreflexia, clonus, Babinski sign
  • Cord compression: Bilateral upper extremity symptoms, gait disturbances
  • Fracture signs: Severe trauma, neurological deficits
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Atlantoaxial instability risk

Range of Motion Testing

Normal Cervical ROM:

  • Flexion: 50-60 degrees
  • Extension: 60-75 degrees
  • Lateral flexion: 45 degrees (each side)
  • Rotation: 80 degrees (each side)

Special Tests

Spurling's Test

Purpose: Detect cervical radiculopathy

Technique: Patient's head is extended and laterally flexed toward the symptomatic side while axial compression is applied

Positive: Reproduction of arm pain in dermatomal distribution

Sensitivity: 0.30 | Specificity: 0.93 | +LR: 4.4

Clinical Pearl: Spurling's test has high specificity but low sensitivity. A positive test strongly suggests cervical radiculopathy, but a negative test doesn't rule it out.

Upper Limb Tension Test (ULTT)

Purpose: Assess neural tension in upper extremity

Technique: Progressive positioning of shoulder, elbow, forearm, and wrist with cervical lateral flexion

ULTT1 (Median nerve bias): Most commonly used

Sensitivity: 0.72 | Specificity: 0.33

Common Cervical Conditions

Cervical Radiculopathy

Compression or irritation of cervical nerve roots, most commonly C6 and C7.

Signs and Symptoms:

  • Dermatomal arm pain
  • Muscle weakness in myotomal pattern
  • Diminished reflexes
  • Sensory changes

Cervicogenic Headache

Headache originating from disorders of the upper cervical spine.

Characteristics:

  • Unilateral headache
  • Associated neck pain
  • Limited cervical ROM
  • Headache triggered by neck movement

  • Perform comprehensive shoulder examination

Shoulder Complex Anatomy

The shoulder complex consists of four joints working together to provide the greatest range of motion of any joint in the human body.

Four Joints of the Shoulder Complex

  • Glenohumeral Joint: True synovial ball-and-socket joint
  • Acromioclavicular Joint: Connects clavicle to acromion
  • Sternoclavicular Joint: Only bony connection of arm to axial skeleton
  • Scapulothoracic Joint: Functional joint between scapula and thorax

Scapulohumeral Rhythm

Normal shoulder abduction requires coordinated movement between the glenohumeral and scapulothoracic joints in approximately a 2:1 ratio.

Figure 15: Shoulder Anatomy
The shoulder complex consists of four joints working together: glenohumeral, acromioclavicular, sternoclavicular, and scapulothoracic joints.
Clinical Pearl: Disruption of normal scapulohumeral rhythm often indicates shoulder pathology and can be observed during active ROM testing.

Rotator Cuff Examination

Supraspinatus Testing

Empty Can Test (Jobe Test)

Purpose: Assess supraspinatus strength and integrity

Technique: Arms at 90° abduction, 30° forward flexion, thumbs down (empty can position)

Sensitivity: 0.89 | Specificity: 0.50

Full Can Test

Alternative: Same position but thumbs up (full can position)

Advantage: Less painful, better isolation of supraspinatus

Infraspinatus/Teres Minor Testing

External Rotation Strength Test

Position: Arm at side, elbow flexed 90°, resist external rotation

Grade: Compare bilateral strength

Subscapularis Testing

Lift-Off Test

Purpose: Assess subscapularis integrity

Technique: Hand behind back, lift off from back against resistance

Sensitivity: 0.18 | Specificity: 0.100

Impingement Tests

Hawkins-Kennedy Test

Purpose: Detect subacromial impingement

Technique: Shoulder and elbow flexed 90°, passively internally rotate shoulder

Sensitivity: 0.79 | Specificity: 0.59 | +LR: 1.9

Neer's Impingement Sign

Technique: Passive forward flexion with scapula stabilized

Sensitivity: 0.89 | Specificity: 0.31

Figure 18: Elbow Joint Anatomy
The elbow joint consists of three articulations: humeroulnar, humeroradial, and proximal radioulnar joints, working together to provide flexion, extension, and forearm rotation.

Common Shoulder Pathologies

Rotator Cuff Tears

Classification:

  • Partial-thickness tears
  • Full-thickness tears
  • Massive tears (>5 cm or involving 2+ tendons)

Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis)

Stages:

  1. Freezing (2-9 months): Progressive pain and stiffness
  2. Frozen (4-12 months): Decreased pain, significant stiffness
  3. Thawing (12-42 months): Gradual return of motion

Capsular Pattern: External rotation > Abduction > Internal rotation

Learning Objectives:
  • Apply evidence-based practice principles to clinical decision making
  • Develop systematic diagnostic reasoning skills
  • Write comprehensive SOAP documentation
  • Create effective treatment goals and discharge plans

Evidence-Based Practice

Evidence-based practice integrates the best research evidence with clinical expertise and patient values to guide clinical decision-making.

The EBP Triad

  • Best Research Evidence: Systematic reviews, RCTs, clinical guidelines
  • Clinical Expertise: Professional judgment and experience
  • Patient Values: Individual preferences, goals, and circumstances

Diagnostic Reasoning Process

Hypothesis-Oriented Clinical Reasoning

  1. Initial Hypothesis Generation: Based on chief complaint and history
  2. Information Gathering: Targeted examination to test hypotheses
  3. Hypothesis Refinement: Modify based on examination findings
  4. Diagnostic Decision: Most probable diagnosis or diagnoses

SOAP Documentation

Subjective (S)

  • Chief complaint and history of present illness
  • Past medical history and medications
  • Functional limitations and goals
  • Pain ratings and behavior

Objective (O)

  • Observation and posture
  • Range of motion measurements
  • Strength testing results
  • Special test findings
  • Functional assessments

Assessment (A)

  • Clinical diagnosis or differential diagnoses
  • Prognosis and rehabilitation potential
  • Problem identification
  • Response to treatment (follow-up visits)

Plan (P)

  • Short-term and long-term goals
  • Treatment interventions
  • Frequency and duration
  • Patient education
  • Re-evaluation timeline
Documentation Pearl: Good documentation serves multiple purposes: communication with other providers, justification for treatment, legal protection, and quality assurance. Make it clear, concise, and clinically relevant.

SMART Goals

Effective treatment goals should be SMART:

  • Specific: Clearly defined outcome
  • Measurable: Quantifiable parameters
  • Achievable: Realistic expectations
  • Relevant: Meaningful to patient function
  • Time-bound: Specific timeframe

Goal Examples

Example Goal: Patient will demonstrate pain-free cervical rotation of 70° bilaterally (compared to current 45° left, 50° right) within 4 weeks to allow safe driving and return to work activities.

Discharge Planning

Effective discharge planning ensures optimal patient outcomes and prevents re-injury:

  • Functional Goals Met: Patient achieves predetermined outcomes
  • Home Exercise Program: Independent maintenance program
  • Activity Modifications: Ergonomic and lifestyle recommendations
  • Follow-up Care: Referrals if needed
  • Return to Activity: Progressive guidelines
