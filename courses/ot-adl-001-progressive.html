Defining Activities of Daily Living

Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) are basic tasks of everyday life. Understanding their hierarchy is fundamental to occupational therapy practice.

Basic ADLs (BADLs)

  • Bathing: Washing body, grooming, oral hygiene
  • Dressing: Selecting clothing, putting on/removing clothes
  • Toileting: Controlling bladder/bowel, using toilet
  • Transferring: Moving between surfaces
  • Eating: Feeding oneself

Instrumental ADLs (IADLs)

  • Meal preparation: Planning, cooking, serving meals
  • Housekeeping: Cleaning, laundry, home maintenance
  • Medication management: Taking medications as prescribed
  • Financial management: Budgeting, paying bills
Clinical Pearl: BADLs are fundamental to survival. IADLs indicate higher-level function. Loss of IADLs often precedes BADL decline.

Functional Independence Measure (FIM)

The FIM is an 18-item standardized measure of physical, psychological, and social function.

FIM ScoreLevelDescription
7Complete IndependenceTimely, safe, no devices
5SupervisionSetup or cueing needed
3Moderate AssistancePatient performs 50-74%
1Total AssistancePatient performs less than 25%

Barthel Index

Assesses 10 basic ADLs on a 0-100 scale. Useful for stroke rehabilitation.

Interpretation: 100 = Fully independent | 60-99 = Mild dependence | 0-19 = Total dependence

Bathing and Grooming Assessment

Bathing and grooming are fundamental self-care activities requiring physical abilities and cognitive awareness.

Components of Bathing Assessment

  • Ability to turn faucets on/off and adjust temperature
  • Safe entry/exit from tub or shower
  • Washing all body parts effectively
  • Drying body thoroughly

Dressing Assessment

Dressing assessment evaluates the patient's ability to select appropriate clothing and put on/remove garments.

Dressing Hierarchy (easiest to hardest): 1. Socks/stockings | 2. Underwear | 3. Pants | 4. Shirt | 5. Shoes | 6. Fasteners

Transfer Assessment

Safe transfers are critical for independence and preventing caregiver injury.

Types of Transfers

  • Bed to chair: Most common transfer
  • Toilet transfers: Requires clothing management
  • Tub/shower transfers: Highest fall risk
  • Car transfers: Community independence
Safety Alert: Always assess fall risk before mobility assessment. Have gait belt ready and ensure clear pathway.

Cognitive Requirements for ADLs

Successful ADL performance requires multiple cognitive domains working together.

Executive Functions Required

  • Planning: Sequencing steps, gathering materials
  • Attention: Sustained focus, task switching
  • Problem-solving: Adapting when things go wrong
  • Memory: Procedural and prospective memory

Managing ADL Deficits in Dementia

As dementia progresses, patients require increasing assistance with ADLs.

Caregiver Education: Simplify tasks, provide cueing, maintain routines, ensure safety, focus on abilities rather than deficits.

Aging and ADL Performance

Normal aging may result in slower performance but does not typically cause loss of ADL independence.

Age-Related Changes Affecting ADLs

  • Decreased strength and endurance
  • Reduced flexibility and range of motion
  • Slower processing speed
  • Visual and hearing changes
  • Balance and coordination changes

Frailty and ADL Decline

Frailty syndrome is a distinct clinical state that increases vulnerability to ADL decline.

Early Intervention: Addressing reversible factors (medication side effects, depression, pain) can restore ADL function even in frail older adults.
