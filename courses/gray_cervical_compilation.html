Gray's Anatomy: Cervical Spine Plates

Henry Gray's Anatomy of the Human Body, 20th Edition (1918)

Source: Public domain images from Gray's Anatomy, 20th edition (1918). Illustrated by Henry Vandyke Carter (1831-1897). These classic anatomical plates represent the gold standard of anatomical illustration from the pre-digital era, known for their exceptional accuracy and clarity.
Plate 85
Side View of a Typical Cervical Vertebra
Lateral view showing the characteristic features of a typical cervical vertebra (C3-C6): small vertebral body, large vertebral foramen, bifid spinous process, and transverse foramina for the vertebral artery.
Clinical Relevance: Understanding the typical cervical vertebra anatomy is essential for interpreting radiographs and identifying pathological changes such as spondylosis, osteophyte formation, and foraminal stenosis.
Plate 86
First Cervical Vertebra — Atlas (C1)
Superior view of the atlas (C1), named after the Greek Titan who held up the world. Note the absence of a vertebral body and spinous process. Features anterior and posterior arches with lateral masses containing superior articular facets for the occipital condyles.
Clinical Relevance: The atlas supports the skull and allows the "yes" nodding motion at the atlanto-occipital joint. Jefferson fractures (burst fractures) involve the atlas and require careful evaluation due to the large spinal canal at this level.
Plate 87
Second Cervical Vertebra — Axis (C2) from Above
Superior view of the axis (C2) showing the distinctive dens (odontoid process) projecting upward. The dens articulates with the anterior arch of the atlas, forming the pivot for rotational movement of the head.
Clinical Relevance: The dens is the pivot for the "no" rotation motion at the atlanto-axial joint. Dens fractures (Type I, II, III) are serious injuries often seen in trauma. The transverse ligament holds the dens against the atlas.
Plate 88
Second Cervical Vertebra — Axis (C2) from the Side
Lateral view of the axis showing the relationship between the dens, vertebral body, and the large bifid spinous process. Note the superior articular facets oriented to face upward and laterally for articulation with the atlas.
Clinical Relevance: The prominent spinous process of C2 is often the first palpable spinous process below the occiput (C1 has no spinous process). Useful landmark for manual therapy and physical examination.
Plate 89
Seventh Cervical Vertebra — Vertebra Prominens (C7)
The seventh cervical vertebra (C7) is called "vertebra prominens" due to its long, prominent non-bifid spinous process. It marks the transition between cervical and thoracic spine. The transverse foramina are smaller and may not transmit the vertebral artery.
Clinical Relevance: C7 is a key landmark — the most prominent spinous process at the base of the neck. Used to count vertebral levels and identify the C7-T1 junction. The spinal nerves at this level contribute to the radial nerve.
