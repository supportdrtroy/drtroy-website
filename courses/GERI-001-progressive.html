Normal vs. Pathological Aging

Understanding normal age-related changes helps differentiate from pathology.

Musculoskeletal System

  • Sarcopenia: 1-2% muscle mass loss per year after age 50
  • Decreased bone density (osteopenia/osteoporosis)
  • Reduced flexibility and joint mobility
  • Slower muscle contraction velocity

Cardiovascular System

  • Decreased maximal heart rate
  • Reduced cardiac output
  • Increased arterial stiffness
  • Orthostatic hypotension common

Nervous System

  • Slower processing speed
  • Reduced proprioception
  • Decreased reaction time
  • Mild cognitive slowing (not dementia)

Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment

A multidisciplinary approach evaluating medical, functional, cognitive, and psychosocial domains.

Key Assessment Components

  • Physical function: ADLs, IADLs, gait, balance, mobility
  • Cognition: Mini-Mental State Exam, Montreal Cognitive Assessment
  • Mood: Geriatric Depression Scale
  • Nutrition: Mini Nutritional Assessment
  • Social support: Caregiver availability, living situation

Frailty Assessment

Frailty is a clinical syndrome of decreased reserve and resistance to stressors.

Fried Frailty CriteriaAssessment
ShrinkingUnintentional weight loss >10 lbs in past year
WeaknessReduced grip strength
ExhaustionSelf-reported poor endurance
SlownessSlow walking speed
Low physical activityReduced energy expenditure
3 or more criteria = Frail; 1-2 criteria = Pre-frail; 0 criteria = Robust

Types of Dementia

TypeCharacteristicsProgression
Alzheimer's DiseaseMemory impairment first, gradual decline8-10 years average
Vascular DementiaStepwise decline, executive dysfunctionVariable
Lewy BodyVisual hallucinations, fluctuations, parkinsonism5-7 years
FrontotemporalBehavioral changes, language deficits early6-8 years

Rehabilitation Considerations

  • Strengths-based approach: Focus on remaining abilities
  • Routine and consistency: Same time, place, sequence
  • Environmental modifications: Reduce distractions, improve lighting
  • Caregiver training: Essential for carryover
  • Safety awareness: Wandering, fall risk, swallowing

Stages of ADL Decline

  1. Complex IADLs (finances, medications, shopping)
  2. Basic IADLs (meal prep, housekeeping)
  3. Basic ADLs (dressing, grooming, toileting)
  4. Feeding, continence
  5. Total dependence

Hip Fracture Types and Repair

Hip fractures are classified by anatomical location and guide surgical intervention.

Fracture TypeLocationTypical Repair
Femoral neckIntracapsular, proximal femurHemiarthroplasty or THA
IntertrochantericExtracapsular, between trochantersSliding hip screw or intramedullary nail
SubtrochantericBelow lesser trochanterIntramedullary nail

Weight-Bearing Status

  • Weight-bearing as tolerated (WBAT): Most common after arthroplasty
  • Toe-touch: May be used with fracture fixation
  • Non-weight bearing: Rare, used for specific complications

Functional Outcomes

  • 50% regain pre-fracture level of function
  • 25% require long-term nursing home care
  • 25% mortality within one year
  • Best outcomes with early mobilization and rehabilitation

Cardinal Features of Parkinson's

  • Tremor at rest: Usually starts in one hand
  • Bradykinesia: Slowness of movement
  • Muscle rigidity: Stiffness in limbs and trunk
  • Postural instability: Late feature (may not be present early)

Rehabilitation Interventions

ImpairmentIntervention
Freezing of gaitVisual cueing (laser cane, floor lines), auditory cueing (metronome)
MicrographiaLSVT BIG, large amplitude movements
HypophoniaLSVT LOUD speech therapy
Flexed posturePostural exercises, spinal extension
Balance deficitsBalance training, multidirectional stepping

Medication Timing

Rehabilitation is most effective when patient is "on" - optimally medicated. Schedule therapy 45-60 minutes after levodopa dose.

What is Polypharmacy?

Generally defined as taking 5 or more medications. Associated with increased risk of adverse drug events, falls, hospitalization, and mortality.

Beers Criteria

List of potentially inappropriate medications for older adults. Updated by American Geriatrics Society.

Medications to Avoid in Older Adults

  • Anticholinergics - confusion, urinary retention, constipation
  • Benzodiazepines - falls, cognitive impairment
  • Non-benzodiazepine hypnotics (Z-drugs) - similar risks
  • Tricyclic antidepressants - anticholinergic effects, cardiac
  • NSAIDs - GI bleeding, renal impairment, cardiovascular risk

Medication-Related Fall Risk

Risk LevelMedication Classes
HighSedatives, antipsychotics, antidepressants, opioids
ModerateAntihypertensives, diuretics, hypoglycemics
LowerMost other medications with proper monitoring

Exercise Prescription for Frailty

Even frail older adults can benefit from exercise. Start low, go slow, but do go.

Progressive Resistance Training

  • Start with body weight or very light resistance
  • Focus on functional movements (sit-to-stand, step-ups)
  • 2-3 sets of 8-12 repetitions
  • Progress by increasing reps before weight

Balance Training

  • Start with seated or supported standing
  • Progress to tandem stance, single-leg stance
  • Include reactive balance training
  • Tai Chi shows excellent results even in frail elders

Multi-component Exercise Programs

Vivifrail and Otago Exercise Programs specifically designed for frail older adults show significant improvements in function and reduced falls.
