Course Information

Course Information

Course Description

This comprehensive continuing education course provides physical therapy professionals with systematic, evidence-based approaches to musculoskeletal evaluation and treatment across all major body regions. Participants will learn examination techniques, special tests with diagnostic accuracy metrics, differential diagnosis strategies, and evidence-informed interventions for the cervical spine, thoracic spine, lumbar spine, sacroiliac joint, shoulder, elbow, wrist/hand, hip, knee, and ankle/foot.

Target Audience

This course is designed for licensed physical therapists seeking to enhance their musculoskeletal examination skills and clinical decision-making abilities.

Course Objectives

Upon completion of this course, participants will be able to:

  1. Perform comprehensive regional musculoskeletal evaluations using systematic, evidence-based examination approaches
  2. Apply and interpret special tests with knowledge of sensitivity, specificity, likelihood ratios, and clinical utility
  3. Identify red flags and recognize when immediate physician referral is indicated
  4. Develop differential diagnoses based on subjective history and objective examination findings
  5. Design evidence-based, region-specific treatment plans tailored to specific pathologies
  6. Integrate examination findings to formulate clinical diagnoses and prognoses
  7. Document findings using appropriate clinical reasoning frameworks

Module 1: Introduction to MSK Evaluation (20 minutes)

1.1 Examination Model

FIGURE 1: Examination Sequence Flowchart (SOAP Format)
Illustration showing the systematic examination process: Subjective (patient history, pain assessment, functional limitations), Objective (observation, ROM, special tests, palpation), Assessment (diagnosis, impairments), and Plan (interventions, goals). Arrows indicate the cyclical nature of reassessment.

The systematic examination follows four phases: Subjective, Objective, Assessment, and Plan (SOAP).

Subjective Examination Components

  • Chief complaint and history of present illness
  • Pain assessment (location, quality, intensity, aggravating/relieving factors)
  • Functional limitations and patient goals
  • Medical history, medications, social history

Objective Examination Components

  • Observation (posture, gait, functional movements)
  • Active and passive range of motion
  • Resisted isometric testing
  • Special tests
  • Palpation
  • Neurological examination when indicated

1.3 Pain Mechanisms

Nociceptive Pain

From actual tissue damage. Localized, mechanical pattern, responds to treatment.

Neuropathic Pain

From nervous system lesion. Burning/shooting, dermatomal distribution, night symptoms.

Nociplastic Pain

Altered pain processing. Widespread, disproportionate, psychosocial factors.

2.3 Special Tests

Spurling's Test (Foraminal Compression)
Purpose: Cervical nerve root compression
Technique: Extend, laterally flex, rotate toward symptomatic side; apply axial compression
Positive: Radicular arm symptoms
Accuracy: Sens 30%, Spec 93% (highly specific)
Contraindications: Severe osteoporosis, fracture, vertebral artery insufficiency
Distraction Test
Purpose: Confirm radiculopathy by relieving compression
Technique: Supine; gently lift head to distract spine
Positive: Reduction of radicular symptoms
Accuracy: Sens 44%, Spec 90%
Vertebral Artery Test
Purpose: Screen before manipulation
Technique: Supine; passively extend, rotate, side-bend; hold 10-30 seconds
Positive: Nystagmus, dizziness, nausea, visual changes, dysarthria
Note: Poor sensitivity but standard precaution

Gray's Anatomy illustration showing typical thoracic vertebra with costal facets for rib articulation. Note the heart-shaped vertebral body and downward-angled spinous process.

3.3 Common Conditions

Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS)

Compression of neurovascular bundle in thoracic outlet.

Roos Test (EAST)
Purpose: Evaluate thoracic outlet syndrome
Technique: Arms abducted 90 degrees, elbows flexed 90 degrees; open/close fists for 3 minutes
Positive: Arm fatigue, numbness, tingling, heaviness

Scheuermann's Disease

Juvenile kyphosis with anterior vertebral wedging >5 degrees in three consecutive vertebrae.

4.3 Special Tests

Straight Leg Raise (SLR)
Purpose: Lumbar disc herniation/radiculopathy
Technique: Supine; passively flex hip with knee straight
Positive: Radicular symptoms 35-70 degrees
Accuracy: Sens 91%, Spec 26%
Note: High sensitivity makes it good screening test
Crossed Straight Leg Raise
Purpose: Lumbar disc herniation (contralateral)
Technique: Lift uninvolved leg; symptoms in involved leg
Positive: Radicular symptoms in opposite leg
Accuracy: Sens 29%, Spec 88% (highly specific)
Slump Test
Purpose: Neural tension/radiculopathy
Technique: Seated; slumped posture, neck flexion, knee extension, ankle dorsiflexion
Positive: Reproduction of symptoms that decrease with neck extension
Accuracy: More sensitive than SLR

Module 5: Sacroiliac Joint (12 minutes)

5.1 Anatomy

FIGURE 14: SI Joint Anatomy
Sacroiliac joint anatomy showing the articulation between the sacrum and ilium. The joint is reinforced by strong ligaments and has limited mobility.
  • Synovial joint with fibrous components
  • 2-4mm of movement normal
  • Weight transfer from spine to pelvis
  • Hormonal influence (pregnancy)

5.2 Special Tests

FABER Test (Patrick's Test)
Purpose: SI joint or hip pathology
Technique: Supine; flexion, abduction, external rotation of hip
Positive: SI joint pain (posterior), hip pain (groin)
Accuracy: Sens 60%, Spec 25% for SI joint dysfunction
Gaenslen's Test
Purpose: SI joint dysfunction
Technique: Supine at table edge; pull knee to chest, extend opposite leg over edge
Positive: SI joint pain on extended side
Accuracy: Moderate specificity when combined with other tests
Compression Test
Purpose: SI joint dysfunction
Technique: Side-lying; compress iliac crests together
Positive: SI joint pain
Note: More specific when multiple SI tests positive
SI Joint Test Cluster: No single SI joint test has high accuracy. Use a cluster approach: if 3+ tests are positive (FABER, Gaenslen's, compression, distraction, posterior shear), the likelihood of SI joint dysfunction increases significantly.

6.3 Special Tests

Impingement Tests

Neer Impingement Sign
Purpose: Subacromial impingement
Technique: Stabilize scapula; passively flex arm overhead
Positive: Pain in subacromial space
Accuracy: Sens 75%, Spec 48%
Hawkins-Kennedy Test
Purpose: Subacromial impingement
Technique: 90 degrees flexion; passively internally rotate
Positive: Pain in subacromial space
Accuracy: Sens 79%, Spec 59%

Rotator Cuff Tests

Empty Can Test (Jobe's Test)
Purpose: Supraspinatus pathology
Technique: 90 degrees abduction, 30 degrees horizontal adduction, thumbs down
Positive: Pain or weakness
Accuracy: Sens 64%, Spec 65%
Drop Arm Test
Purpose: Full-thickness rotator cuff tear
Technique: Patient lowers arm slowly from 90 degrees abduction
Positive: Arm drops or severe pain
Accuracy: Sens 21%, Spec 92% (highly specific)
Impingement Test Cluster: Combined positive Neer and Hawkins-Kennedy tests significantly increase the likelihood of subacromial impingement syndrome.

8.3 Special Tests

Phalen's Test
Purpose: Carpal tunnel syndrome
Technique: Wrists in full flexion for 60 seconds
Positive: Numbness/tingling in median nerve distribution
Accuracy: Sens 68%, Spec 73%
Tinel's Sign
Purpose: Carpal tunnel syndrome
Technique: Tap over median nerve at carpal tunnel
Positive: Tingling in median nerve distribution
Accuracy: Sens 50%, Spec 77%
Finkelstein's Test
Purpose: De Quervain's tenosynovitis
Technique: Thumb in fist; ulnar deviate wrist
Positive: Pain over radial styloid
Accuracy: High specificity for De Quervain's
Carpal Tunnel Diagnosis: Combine clinical history (night symptoms, thenar weakness) with physical findings. Positive Phalen's and Tinel's together increase diagnostic confidence.

9.3 Special Tests

FADIR Test
Purpose: Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI)
Technique: Supine; hip flexion, adduction, internal rotation
Positive: Groin pain
Accuracy: Sens 94-99%, most sensitive test for FAI
FABER Test
Purpose: Hip or SI joint pathology
Technique: Flexion, abduction, external rotation
Positive: Groin pain (hip), posterior pain (SI joint)
Note: Localizes to different structures based on pain location
Thomas Test
Purpose: Hip flexor tightness
Technique: Supine; pull knee to chest, observe opposite leg
Positive: Opposite leg lifts off table
Note: Differentiates iliopsoas vs rectus femoris tightness
Trendelenburg Test
Purpose: Hip abductor strength (gluteus medius)
Technique: Single leg stance for 30 seconds
Positive: Pelvis drops on opposite side
Note: Indicates weakness of stance leg abductors

9.4 Common Conditions

Hip Osteoarthritis

Presents with groin pain, morning stiffness, and functional limitations. Often associated with capsular pattern of restriction.

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI)

Abnormal contact between femoral head and acetabulum. Two types: Cam (aspherical femoral head) and Pincer (acetabular overcoverage).

Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome

Previously called trochanteric bursitis. More often tendinopathy of gluteus medius/minimus insertions.

10.3 Ligament Tests

ACL Tests

Lachman Test
Purpose: ACL integrity (gold standard)
Technique: 30 degrees knee flexion; anterior drawer of tibia
Positive: Excessive anterior translation, soft endpoint
Accuracy: Sens 85%, Spec 94%
Anterior Drawer Test
Purpose: ACL integrity
Technique: 90 degrees knee flexion; anterior drawer of tibia
Positive: Excessive anterior translation
Accuracy: Less sensitive than Lachman due to hamstring guarding

PCL Test

Posterior Drawer Test
Purpose: PCL integrity
Technique: 90 degrees knee flexion; posterior drawer of tibia
Positive: Excessive posterior translation
Note: Look for posterior sag sign first

Collateral Ligament Tests

Valgus Stress Test (MCL)
Purpose: MCL integrity
Technique: 0 and 30 degrees knee flexion; apply valgus stress
Positive: Excessive medial opening
Note: Test at 30 degrees isolates MCL
Varus Stress Test (LCL)
Purpose: LCL integrity
Technique: 0 and 30 degrees knee flexion; apply varus stress
Positive: Excessive lateral opening
Note: LCL injuries less common than MCL

10.4 Meniscal Tests

McMurray Test
Purpose: Meniscal tear
Technique: Supine; flex knee to chest, rotate tibia, extend knee
Positive: Click or pain during maneuver
Accuracy: Sens 52%, Spec 97% (highly specific)
Thessaly Test
Purpose: Meniscal pathology
Technique: Standing on one leg, rotate body while maintaining foot position
Positive: Medial/lateral joint line pain
Accuracy: Sens 90%, Spec 97%

10.5 Patellofemoral Tests

Patellar Grind Test
Purpose: Patellofemoral joint pathology
Technique: Supine; quadriceps relaxed; compress patella into trochlea while patient attempts quad set
Positive: Pain under patella
Q-Angle Assessment
Purpose: Patellofemoral alignment
Technique: Measure angle between line from ASIS to patella center, and patella center to tibial tubercle
Normal: Males 8-14 degrees, Females 12-18 degrees
Significance: Increased angle associated with patellar maltracking

11.4 Ottawa Ankle Rules

Order X-rays if:

  • Inability to bear weight immediately and in emergency department (4 steps)
  • Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of lateral malleolus
  • Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of medial malleolus
  • Bone tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal
  • Bone tenderness at navicular
Ottawa Rules Accuracy: Nearly 100% sensitive for fracture. Reduces unnecessary X-rays by ~30-40% while maintaining diagnostic accuracy.

Module 12: Clinical Reasoning and Documentation (35 minutes)

12.1 Evidence-Based Practice (EBP)

Evidence-based practice integrates three essential components to optimize patient outcomes:

The EBP Triad

  • Best Research Evidence: Systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials, clinical practice guidelines
  • Clinical Expertise: Your experience, training, and professional judgment
  • Patient Values: Patient preferences, expectations, and circumstances
Hierarchy of Evidence: Systematic reviews and meta-analyses provide the highest level of evidence, followed by RCTs, cohort studies, case-control studies, case series, and expert opinion. Clinical practice guidelines synthesize evidence for specific conditions.

12.2 Diagnostic Reasoning

The Hypothesis-Oriented Approach

  1. Generate Hypotheses: Based on subjective history, create a list of possible diagnoses
  2. Select Tests: Choose examination procedures that differentiate between hypotheses
  3. Test Hypotheses: Perform objective tests and interpret findings
  4. Refine or Confirm: Update your diagnostic impression based on results
  5. Reassess: Continuously evaluate as new information emerges

12.3 Likelihood Ratios and Diagnostic Accuracy

Likelihood ratios (LRs) quantify how much a test result changes the probability of a condition:

Interpreting Likelihood Ratios

LR+ ValueEffect on ProbabilityClinical Interpretation
>10Large increaseTest essentially rules in the condition
5-10Moderate increaseSubstantially increases probability
2-5Small increaseSomewhat increases probability
1-2Minimal increaseRarely changes clinical decision
Cluster Testing: No single test has perfect accuracy. Using clusters of tests with positive likelihood ratios improves diagnostic confidence. The more positive findings in a cluster, the higher the post-test probability.

12.4 SMART Goals Framework

Well-written goals guide treatment and measure progress:

SMART Criteria Explained

  • Specific: Clearly defined, not vague. "Walk 1 mile" vs. "Improve walking"
  • Measurable: Quantifiable outcome. "Knee flexion to 120 degrees" vs. "Better knee bend"
  • Achievable: Realistic given condition and timeframe. Consider tissue healing times
  • Relevant: Meaningful to the patient's life and priorities
  • Time-bound: Specific deadline for achievement. "Within 4 weeks"

12.5 Documentation: The SOAP Note

The SOAP format provides a structured, legally defensible record of patient care:

Subjective (S)

Document the patient's report in their own words when possible:

  • Chief Complaint: Primary reason for visit
  • Pain: Location, quality, intensity (VAS 0-10), pattern
  • Functional Status: What they can/cannot do
  • Patient Goals: What they want to achieve

Objective (O)

Record measurable, observable findings:

  • Observation: Posture, gait, swelling, atrophy
  • ROM: Active and passive, with end-feel
  • Strength: MMT grades (0-5) or dynamometry
  • Special Tests: Positive and negative findings with interpretation

Assessment (A)

Synthesize findings into clinical conclusions:

  • Diagnosis/Impression: Primary and secondary diagnoses
  • Impairments: Specific limitations identified
  • Prognosis: Expected outcome and timeline

Plan (P)

Outline the treatment approach and next steps:

  • Interventions: Specific treatments provided today
  • Patient Education: What was taught
  • Goals: Short-term and long-term with timelines
  • Follow-up: Next visit frequency and plan
