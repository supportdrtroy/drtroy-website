## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Perform systematic shoulder examination using evidence-based techniques - Differentiate between rotator cuff pathology, impingement, and instability - Apply clinical decision-making principles for shoulder pain management - Recognize when imaging or specialist referral is indicated
**Troy's Practice Pearl:** "Before touching the shoulder, always clear the cervical spine. I've seen too many 'shoulder impingements' that were actually C6 radiculopathies. A quick Spurling test can save you from treating the wrong structure."
## Case Introduction: Meet Sarah **Patient:** 45-year-old administrative assistant **Chief Complaint:** "My shoulder hurts when I reach for things on high shelves" **History:** 3-month gradual onset, no trauma, works at computer 8 hours/day *We'll follow Sarah's case throughout this module to demonstrate systematic evaluation* ## Systematic Examination Framework ### Step 1: Observation and Posture - **Forward head posture** (common in office workers) - **Rounded shoulders** with protracted scapulae - **Shoulder height asymmetry**
**Clinical Insight:** Forward head posture places the cervical spine in extension and can create referred shoulder pain. Always address postural factors in shoulder rehabilitation.
### Step 2: Active Range of Motion Test in this order to identify patterns: | Movement | Normal Range | Sarah's Values | Clinical Significance | |----------|--------------|----------------|---------------------| | Flexion | 150-180° | 160° | Slight limitation | | Abduction | 150-180° | 145° with pain arc | Suggests impingement | | External Rotation | 45-60° | 50° | Within normal limits | | Internal Rotation | T7-T10 | T12 | Significant limitation |
**Red Flag Alert:** If active ROM is severely limited in ALL directions with significant pain, consider: - Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) - Septic arthritis (fever, severe pain) - Fracture (trauma history)
### Step 3: Passive Range of Motion - **Rule:** Passive > Active suggests muscle weakness - **Rule:** Passive = Active suggests capsular restriction *In Sarah's case: Passive ROM gains 10-15° in all directions, suggesting muscle guarding rather than capsular restriction* ### Step 4: Strength Testing #### Rotator Cuff Assessment Battery
**Empty Can Test - Supraspinatus** - **Technique:** 90° abduction, 30° forward flexion, thumbs down - **Normal:** 5/5 strength without pain - **Sarah's Result:** 4/5 with pain at lateral acromion - **Interpretation:** Possible supraspinatus tendinopathy **Evidence:** - Sensitivity: 64% - Specificity: 65% - +LR: 1.8 (modest increase in probability) **Clinical Pearl:** The empty can test is often positive in impingement syndrome even without rotator cuff tear. Use in combination with other tests.
**External Rotation Lag Sign - Infraspinatus** - **Technique:** Arm at side, elbow 90°, passively externally rotate, release - **Sarah's Result:** NEGATIVE (maintains position) - **Significance:** Rules out full-thickness infraspinatus tear **Evidence:** - Sensitivity: 70% - Specificity: 100% - **When positive:** Pathognomonic for full-thickness tear
### Step 5: Special Tests - Impingement Battery
**Hawkins-Kennedy Test** - **Technique:** 90° shoulder flexion, forced internal rotation - **Sarah's Result:** POSITIVE (reproduces familiar pain) - **Interpretation:** Supports impingement hypothesis **Evidence:** - Sensitivity: 79% - Specificity: 59% - Most sensitive test for subacromial impingement
**Neer Impingement Sign** - **Technique:** Passive elevation with scapula stabilized - **Sarah's Result:** POSITIVE (pain in impingement arc 60-120°) **Troy's Technique Tip:** "I stabilize the scapula firmly with my hand. Many clinicians miss this step, which reduces the test's effectiveness."
## Clinical Reasoning Integration ### Sarah's Assessment Summary: - **History:** Gradual onset, overhead activities, office worker - **Examination:** Limited abduction with painful arc, positive impingement signs - **Strength:** Supraspinatus weakness without lag signs - **Differential Diagnosis:** Subacromial impingement syndrome ### Treatment Direction Indicators: ✅ **Conservative management appropriate** ✅ **Address postural factors** ✅ **Strengthening program indicated** ❌ **Imaging not immediately necessary**
**Decision Point: When to Order Imaging?** Consider MRI if: - No improvement after 6-8 weeks of conservative care - Positive lag signs suggesting full-thickness tear - Age >60 with significant weakness - Failed steroid injection - Patient requests surgical consultation **For Sarah:** Trial conservative management for 6-8 weeks first
## Clinical Decision Making ### Probability Analysis for Sarah: **Pre-test probability of subacromial impingement:** HIGH - Age 45 (peak incidence 40-60) - Overhead activity symptoms - Gradual onset - Office worker posture **Post-test probability after examination:** VERY HIGH - Multiple positive impingement tests (+LR compounds) - Negative lag signs rule out competing diagnosis - Typical presentation pattern ### Treatment Planning **Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2):** Pain and inflammation control - Activity modification - Postural correction - Gentle ROM exercises **Phase 2 (Weeks 3-6):** Strength and function - Rotator cuff strengthening - Scapular stabilization - Progressive loading **Phase 3 (Weeks 6+):** Return to activities - Functional movements - Workplace ergonomics - Prevention strategies ## Common Clinical Mistakes
**Mistake #1:** Testing only the symptomatic shoulder **Solution:** Always compare bilaterally for subtle differences **Mistake #2:** Performing special tests without clearing cervical spine **Solution:** Quick Spurling test should precede shoulder examination **Mistake #3:** Over-relying on single positive test **Solution:** Use test clusters and clinical reasoning **Mistake #4:** Ignoring postural contributions **Solution:** Address thoracic kyphosis and forward head posture
