The Burden of Falls

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death among adults 65 and older in the United States.

Key Statistics

  • One in four older adults falls each year
  • More than 3 million falls treated in emergency departments annually
  • Over 800,000 hospitalizations due to fall injuries
  • Over 42,000 fall-related deaths in 2020
  • Fall-related costs exceed $50 billion annually

Consequences of Falls

  • Physical: Fractures (hip, wrist, vertebral), traumatic brain injury, soft tissue injury
  • Psychological: Fear of falling, loss of confidence, depression, social isolation
  • Functional: Loss of independence, disability, nursing home placement
  • Economic: Medical costs, rehabilitation, long-term care

Sensory Systems for Balance

Postural control relies on integration of three sensory systems:

1. Visual System

  • Provides information about environment and self-motion
  • Essential for anticipatory postural adjustments
  • Declines with age: reduced acuity, contrast sensitivity, depth perception

2. Vestibular System

  • Detects head position and movement
  • Contributes to gaze stability and postural control
  • Common age-related decline: BPPV, reduced vestibular hair cells

3. Somatosensory System

  • Proprioception from joints and muscles
  • Cutaneous sensation from feet
  • Often impaired in diabetes, peripheral neuropathy
Clinical Pearl: The Romberg test assesses balance with eyes closed to remove visual input. Increased sway with eyes closed suggests somatosensory or vestibular deficit.

Intrinsic Risk Factors

Internal factors related to the individual's health and function.

CategoryExamples
Age-related changesReduced vision, hearing, proprioception, reaction time
Medical conditionsStroke, Parkinson's, arthritis, diabetes, hypotension
MedicationsSedatives, antidepressants, antihypertensives, diuretics
Cognitive impairmentDementia, delirium, poor judgment
Mobility limitationsWeakness, gait impairment, balance deficits
PsychologicalDepression, fear of falling, anxiety

Extrinsic Risk Factors

Environmental and external factors.

  • Poor lighting, glare
  • Slippery or uneven surfaces
  • Throw rugs and loose carpets
  • Lack of grab bars in bathroom
  • Cluttered pathways
  • Improper footwear

Timed Up and Go (TUG)

Measures functional mobility and fall risk. Patient stands from chair, walks 10 feet, turns, returns to chair.

  • Normal: Less than 10 seconds
  • Fall risk: Greater than 12 seconds
  • High fall risk: Greater than 20 seconds

Berg Balance Scale

14-item performance-based measure of static and dynamic balance. Maximum score 56.

  • 41-56: Low fall risk
  • 21-40: Medium fall risk
  • 0-20: High fall risk

Functional Reach Test

Assesses stability by measuring maximum forward reach while standing.

  • Less than 10 inches: High fall risk
  • 10-12 inches: Moderate risk
  • Greater than 12 inches: Low risk

Morse Fall Scale

Used in hospitals to assess fall risk. Score 0-24: low risk; 25-50: moderate risk; over 50: high risk.

Home Safety Interventions

Environmental modifications can reduce falls by up to 20% when combined with education.

Bathroom Modifications

  • Install grab bars in shower and near toilet
  • Use non-slip mats in tub/shower
  • Add shower chair or bench
  • Install raised toilet seat
  • Ensure adequate lighting

General Home Modifications

  • Remove throw rugs or secure with non-slip backing
  • Improve lighting - add nightlights, increase wattage
  • Clear clutter from walkways
  • Secure loose carpets and cords
  • Install handrails on both sides of stairs
  • Mark step edges with contrasting tape

Footwear Recommendations

  • Well-fitting shoes with low heels
  • Non-slip soles
  • Thin, firm midsoles for proprioception
  • Avoid walking in socks or stockings

Falls in Neurological Conditions

Parkinson's Disease

  • Freezing of gait is major fall risk
  • Visual cueing strategies (laser canes, floor lines)
  • Amplitude-based movement training (LSVT BIG)

Stroke

  • Hemiparesis affects weight-bearing and balance
  • Hemineglect increases environmental hazards
  • Task-specific training for real-world activities

Diabetes and Peripheral Neuropathy

  • Loss of protective sensation in feet
  • Visual compensatory strategies needed
  • Proper footwear essential

Fear of Falling

Fear of falling is independent risk factor for future falls. Activity restriction leads to deconditioning and increased risk.

Intervention: Cognitive behavioral therapy, graded exposure to challenging activities, and balance confidence building exercises help reduce fear of falling.
