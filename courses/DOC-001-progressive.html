Documentation as a Legal Record

Clinical documentation serves as the legal record of care provided and is discoverable in litigation.

Key Legal Principles

  • If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done: Absence of documentation implies absence of care
  • Clear and legible: Illegible entries may be interpreted against the provider
  • Timely: Late entries should be clearly labeled as such
  • Accurate: Corrections must be made properly without obliterating original text
  • Complete: All required elements must be present

Common Documentation Errors

  • Copying and pasting without updating information
  • Using vague or subjective language
  • Inconsistent information across notes
  • Missing signatures or credentials
  • Late or retroactive entries
Legal Alert: Altering records after a patient complaint or lawsuit is filed constitutes fraud and can result in loss of license.

SOAP Format

SOAP notes provide organized, consistent documentation structure.

Subjective (S)

  • Patient's report of symptoms, pain, function
  • Direct quotes when relevant
  • Includes prior level of function and current limitations

Objective (O)

  • Measurable, observable data
  • Vital signs, range of motion, strength measurements
  • Functional assessments and observations

Assessment (A)

  • Clinical interpretation of subjective and objective data
  • Progress toward goals
  • Changes in functional status

Plan (P)

  • Interventions performed
  • Response to treatment
  • Next session plan and modifications

Medicare Part B Documentation

Medicare has specific requirements for therapy services to be reimbursable.

Requirements for Initial Evaluation

  • History of present condition
  • Prior level of function
  • Standardized tests and measures
  • Clinical decision-making for diagnosis
  • Plan of care with measurable goals
  • Signature and date

Treatment Note Requirements

  • Date of service
  • Interventions performed (specific, not generic)
  • Time spent on each intervention
  • Patient's response to treatment
  • Skilled service justification
Medicare Requirement: Documentation must support that services are "reasonable and necessary" and require the skilled intervention of a therapist.

SMART Goal Framework

Goals must be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

Components of a Well-Written Goal

  • Who: The patient
  • Will do what: Specific functional task or activity
  • Under what conditions: Context of performance
  • To what level: Measurable criteria for success
  • By when: Timeframe for achievement

Examples

Poor GoalImproved SMART Goal
Patient will improve walking.Patient will ambulate 150 feet with rolling walker on level surfaces without loss of balance within 2 weeks.
Patient will get stronger.Patient will perform sit-to-stand from standard chair without upper extremity support for 5 repetitions within 3 weeks.
Patient will have less pain.Patient will report pain level of 3/10 or less during dressing activities within 1 week.

Progress Report Requirements

Regular progress reports document ongoing need for therapy and response to treatment.

Required Elements

  • Comparison of current status to initial evaluation and prior progress reports
  • Progress toward goals (specific, objective data)
  • Changes in clinical status and functional abilities
  • Barriers to progress if applicable
  • Assessment of continued need for therapy
  • Revised goals if indicated
  • Plan for continued treatment or discharge

Re-evaluation vs. Progress Note

Progress NoteRe-evaluation
Summary of treatment sessionsComplete reassessment of functional status
Documents response to interventionsMay include new tests and measures
Can be daily or weeklyRequired at minimum every 10 visits or 30 days

Discharge Summary Requirements

Discharge documentation summarizes the episode of care and justifies the reason for ending services.

Discharge Summary Components

  • Dates of treatment (first and last visit)
  • Total number of treatment sessions
  • Current functional status compared to initial
  • Goals status (met, partially met, not met with explanation)
  • Reason for discharge
  • Recommendations for continued care (home program, community resources)
  • Equipment provided or recommended
  • Patient and caregiver education provided

Reasons for Discharge

  • Goals achieved
  • No longer making progress (plateau)
  • No longer needs skilled services
  • Patient refusal or non-compliance
  • Medical instability or hospitalization
  • Patient relocation

Understanding denial reasons helps prevent future denials and supports successful appeals.

Denial ReasonPrevention Strategy
Missing or incomplete documentationUse checklists; thorough initial evaluations
Lack of medical necessityClear justification for skilled services
Maintenance therapyDocument progressive treatment and patient response
Untimed code billing errorsEnsure correct units based on time
Missing signaturesPrompt co-signature for assistants

Appeals Process

  • Redetermination: First level, reviewed by same Medicare contractor
  • Reconsideration: Second level, independent review
  • Administrative Law Judge: Third level for amounts over $160
  • Medicare Appeals Council: Fourth level
  • Federal Court: Fifth level

EHR Documentation Best Practices

Electronic documentation offers efficiency but requires specific safeguards.

EHR Advantages

  • Legibility
  • Template use for efficiency
  • Automatic coding assistance
  • Clinical decision support
  • Easy retrieval and reporting

EHR Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Copy-forward errors: Not updating information carried forward
  • Template overuse: Generic entries that don't reflect actual care
  • Autocorrect errors: Wrong word substitutions
  • Note bloat: Excessive information obscuring relevant content
  • Over-reliance on templates: Missing unique patient factors
Audit Tip: Medicare contractors look for identical entries across multiple dates of service as potential copy-paste abuse.
