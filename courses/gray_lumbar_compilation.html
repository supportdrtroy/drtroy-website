Gray's Anatomy: Lumbar Spine Plates

Henry Gray's Anatomy of the Human Body, 20th Edition (1918)

Source: Public domain images from Gray's Anatomy, 20th edition (1918). Illustrated by Henry Vandyke Carter (1831-1897). These classic anatomical plates represent the gold standard of anatomical illustration from the pre-digital era, known for their exceptional accuracy and clarity.
Plate 92
A Lumbar Vertebra Seen from the Side
Lateral view of a lumbar vertebra showing the massive kidney-shaped vertebral body designed to bear significant weight. Note the long, sturdy spinous process projecting posteriorly and the thick transverse processes extending laterally. The superior and inferior articular processes form the facet joints.
Clinical Relevance: The lumbar vertebrae bear the greatest load in the spine. The large vertebral bodies and thick pedicles provide structural strength. The orientation of facet joints (sagittal plane) favors flexion/extension while limiting rotation.
Plate 93
A Lumbar Vertebra from Above and Behind
Superior-posterior oblique view showing the vertebral arch structures: the thick pedicles connecting to the broad laminae, the triangular vertebral foramen (larger than thoracic, smaller than cervical), and the articular processes with their cartilaginous facets.
Clinical Relevance: The lumbar spinal canal houses the cauda equina (nerve roots below L1-L2). The large pedicles are targets for pedicle screw fixation in spinal fusion surgery. The pars interarticularis is the site of spondylolysis.
Plate 94
A Lumbar Vertebra from Above
Superior view of a lumbar vertebra showing the large, kidney-shaped vertebral body with its superior surface. The vertebral arch forms the posterior boundary of the vertebral foramen. Note the orientation of the superior articular facets facing medially (inward) to articulate with the inferior facets of the vertebra above.
Clinical Relevance: The superior view reveals the facet orientation that guides lumbar movement. The broad laminae provide attachment for the ligamentum flavum. The large central canal accommodates the cauda equina before nerve roots exit through the intervertebral foramina.
