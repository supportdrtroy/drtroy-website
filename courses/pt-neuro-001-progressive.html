1.1 The Neurological Examination Framework

A comprehensive neurological assessment requires systematic evaluation of mental status, cranial nerves, motor function, sensory systems, reflexes, coordination, and gait. The framework ensures no critical findings are missed and provides a baseline for tracking recovery.

Core Components of Neurological Assessment:
  • Mental Status: Level of consciousness (Glasgow Coma Scale), orientation, attention, memory, language
  • Cranial Nerves: CN I-XII systematic screening for brainstem and peripheral nerve integrity
  • Motor Examination: Tone, strength (MMT), involuntary movements, coordination
  • Sensory Examination: Light touch, pain/temperature, proprioception, vibration, cortical sensation
  • Reflexes: Deep tendon reflexes, pathological reflexes (Babinski, Hoffmann's)
  • Cerebellar Function: Finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin, rapid alternating movements
  • Gait and Balance: Observation, Romberg, tandem walking, functional mobility

Distinguishing between UMN and LMN involvement is fundamental to neurological diagnosis and guides intervention planning.

1.2 Standardized Neurological Assessment Tools

Cognitive and Consciousness Scales

Essential Assessment Instruments:
  • Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS): Eye opening (4), verbal response (5), motor response (6) ‚Äî Total 3-15
  • Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE): 30-point screening for cognitive impairment (orientation, registration, attention, recall, language)
  • Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA): More sensitive than MMSE for mild cognitive impairment; includes executive function testing
  • Rancho Los Amigos Scale: Levels I-VIII for cognitive functioning after TBI

Functional Motor Scales

  • Fugl-Meyer Assessment (FMA): Gold standard for post-stroke motor recovery; sensorimotor, balance, joint motion, pain (226 points total)
  • Berg Balance Scale (BBS): 14-item functional balance assessment (0-56 points); scores <45 predict fall risk
  • Functional Independence Measure (FIM): 18 items across motor (13) and cognitive (5) domains; 7-point ordinal scale per item
  • Modified Ashworth Scale: 6-point scale (0-4) for spasticity grading during passive movement
  • National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS): 15-item stroke severity scale; guides acute treatment decisions

1.3 Clinical Reasoning in Neurological Conditions

Neurological clinical reasoning differs from musculoskeletal reasoning in its emphasis on lesion localization, neural pathway analysis, and understanding of neuroplasticity principles that guide recovery expectations.

Red Flags in Neurological Assessment:
  • Sudden severe headache ("thunderclap") ‚Äî possible subarachnoid hemorrhage ‚Üí EMERGENCY
  • Acute onset focal neurological deficits ‚Äî possible stroke ‚Üí activate stroke protocol
  • Progressive bilateral weakness ‚Äî possible Guillain-Barr√© syndrome ‚Üí urgent referral
  • New onset seizures ‚Äî requires immediate medical evaluation
  • Signs of increased intracranial pressure (headache, vomiting, papilledema) ‚Üí EMERGENCY

2.1 Stroke Classification and Pathophysiology

Stroke is the leading cause of long-term disability in adults. Understanding stroke subtypes and vascular territories is essential for predicting deficits and planning rehabilitation.

Stroke Classification:
  • Ischemic Stroke (87%): Thrombotic (large vessel atherosclerosis), embolic (cardiac source), lacunar (small vessel disease)
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke (13%): Intracerebral hemorrhage (hypertensive), subarachnoid hemorrhage (aneurysmal)

2.2 Neuroplasticity and Recovery Mechanisms

Neuroplasticity ‚Äî the brain's capacity to reorganize neural networks ‚Äî is the biological basis for stroke rehabilitation. Understanding these mechanisms informs evidence-based treatment intensity, timing, and approaches.

Principles of Experience-Dependent Neuroplasticity:
  • Use it or lose it: Neural circuits not actively engaged undergo degradation
  • Use it and improve it: Training drives cortical reorganization specific to the function trained
  • Specificity: Nature of training determines nature of plasticity
  • Repetition matters: Sufficient repetition is required to induce lasting neural changes
  • Intensity matters: Training must be of sufficient intensity to induce plasticity
  • Time matters: Different forms of plasticity occur at different times during training
  • Salience matters: Training must be meaningful and motivating to the patient
  • Transference: Plasticity in one set of neural circuits can promote concurrent plasticity in related circuits
  • Interference: Maladaptive plasticity can occur (learned nonuse)

2.3 Evidence-Based Stroke Interventions

Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy (CIMT)

CIMT involves restraining the unaffected extremity while intensively training the affected limb. Strong evidence (Level I) supports its effectiveness for improving upper extremity function in patients with some active wrist and finger extension.

  • Traditional protocol: Mitt on unaffected hand 90% of waking hours + 6 hours/day structured practice for 2 weeks
  • Modified CIMT: Reduced intensity (3 hours/day, mitt 5 hours/day) ‚Äî more feasible in clinical settings
  • Inclusion criteria: Minimum 20¬∞ wrist extension, 10¬∞ finger extension active movement

Task-Specific Training

Practice of functional tasks that are meaningful to the patient, incorporating high repetitions and progressive challenge. Current evidence recommends 300-400+ repetitions per session for motor learning.

Body Weight-Supported Treadmill Training (BWSTT)

Allows early gait training with partial body weight support. Evidence supports use in subacute stroke for improving walking speed and endurance. Recommended starting at 30-40% body weight support with progressive reduction.

Clinical Consideration: The critical window for maximum neuroplasticity is the first 3-6 months post-stroke. However, meaningful recovery can continue well beyond this period with appropriate intervention intensity and specificity.

3.1 Upper Extremity Rehabilitation Post-Stroke

Upper extremity recovery after stroke is often more limited than lower extremity recovery. Only 12-34% of stroke survivors achieve full arm function recovery, making targeted interventions critical.

Evidence-Based UE Interventions (AHA/ASA Guidelines):
  • Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy: Strong evidence (Class I) for chronic stroke with some residual hand function
  • Robot-Assisted Therapy: Moderate evidence for improving motor recovery, especially in proximal UE
  • Mental Practice/Motor Imagery: Adjunct to physical practice; activates similar cortical areas as actual movement
  • Mirror Therapy: Visual illusion of normal movement activates mirror neuron system; moderate evidence for motor and ADL improvement
  • Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES): Augments voluntary movement; strongest evidence for wrist/finger extension and shoulder subluxation
  • Virtual Reality Training: Engaging, motivating environment for repetitive practice; emerging evidence

Shoulder Complications Post-Stroke

Hemiplegic shoulder pain affects 22-84% of stroke survivors and significantly impairs rehabilitation participation and quality of life.

  • Glenohumeral subluxation: Manage with proper positioning, arm troughs, slings (Bobath sling, GivMohr), FES to supraspinatus/posterior deltoid
  • Shoulder-hand syndrome (CRPS Type I): Early recognition critical; edema, pain, vasomotor changes. Manage with elevation, retrograde massage, gentle AROM
  • Rotator cuff injury: Avoid overhead pulleys; use pain-free ROM, scapular stabilization

3.2 Lower Extremity and Gait Rehabilitation

Gait Deviations After Stroke and Interventions:
  • Circumduction: Compensatory pattern for inability to flex hip/knee ‚Äî address hip flexor strengthening, FES for dorsiflexion
  • Knee hyperextension (recurvatum): Quadriceps weakness or spasticity ‚Äî eccentric quad training, hinged AFO
  • Foot drop: Ankle dorsiflexor weakness ‚Äî FES, AFO, tibialis anterior strengthening
  • Trendelenburg gait: Hip abductor weakness ‚Äî gluteus medius strengthening, lateral weight shifting

Locomotor Training Approaches

  • Overground gait training: Functional, task-specific; use cueing strategies for symmetry
  • Body weight-supported treadmill training: Start at 30-40% BWS, reduce progressively; strong evidence for improving speed and endurance
  • Robotic gait training (Lokomat, Ekso): Provides consistent, high-repetition stepping; beneficial for patients unable to achieve voluntary stepping
  • Community ambulation training: Address dual-task walking, uneven surfaces, curbs, ramps for real-world function

  • Follows 2-step commands consistently

Motor Function:
  • Right hemiparesis

  • LE: Hip 3/5, knee 3/5, ankle 2/5
  • Modified Ashworth Scale: UE 1+, LE 1
Functional Status:

  • Transfers: Moderate assist with sliding board
  • Bed mobility: Minimal assist, uses bed rail
  • Balance: Unsupported sitting <30 seconds
Sensation & Perception:
  • Diminished light touch right arm/hand
  • Intact proprioception LE
  • No visual field deficits
  • Mild right-sided neglect

Clinical Reasoning & Analysis:

1. Stroke Classification & Prognosis:

Left MCA territory stroke with classic presentation: right hemiparesis greater in UE than LE, expressive aphasia, and mild neglect. Cardioembolic mechanism (atrial fibrillation) suggests complete vessel occlusion rather than watershed infarct, supporting potential for good recovery if reperfusion occurred.

2. Brunnstrom Stage Assessment:

UE presents at Brunnstrom Stage 2 (emerging spasticity, minimal voluntary movement). LE shows Stage 3 characteristics (voluntary movement within synergy patterns). This suggests faster LE recovery potential, typical in MCA strokes.

3. Rehabilitation Priorities:

Focus on trunk control and sitting balance as foundation. Early weight-bearing activities for LE strengthening. UE interventions should emphasize scapular stability and proximal control before addressing distal function.

Evidence-Based Intervention Plan:

Phase 1: Acute (Days 5-14)
  • Mobility: Progressive sitting balance, bed mobility training, early supported standing
  • UE: Passive ROM, scapular mobilization, weight-bearing activities on stable surface
  • LE: Progressive weight-bearing, pre-gait activities, ankle pumps for DVT prevention
  • Functional: Feeding with adaptive equipment, grooming with setup
Phase 2: Subacute (Weeks 2-12)
  • Gait Training: Body weight-supported treadmill training starting 30% BWS
  • UE Recovery: Task-specific reaching, bilateral activities, mirror therapy
  • Balance: Sitting balance progression, standing balance with support
  • ADL Focus: Dressing techniques, bathroom transfers, kitchen mobility

6-Week Outcomes:

Motor Recovery:
  • UE: Brunnstrom Stage 4 (movement outside synergy patterns emerging)
  • LE: Brunnstrom Stage 5 (independent joint movements)
  • Walks 150 feet with quad cane and AFO
Functional Independence:
  • FIM Motor: 72/91 (modified independence most tasks)
  • Transfers: Independent with equipment
  • ADLs: Independent dressing, minimal assist bathing

Clinical Discussion Points:

  1. Recovery Pattern: Why did LE recovery exceed UE recovery? Discuss corticospinal tract organization and alternative motor pathways.
  2. Intervention Timing: How did early mobilization within 24-48 hours contribute to outcomes?
  3. Prognostic Factors: Which initial assessment findings best predicted functional recovery?
  4. Discharge Planning: What community resources and equipment will Margaret need for safe home discharge?

Traumatic brain injury affects approximately 1.7 million Americans annually. Classification by severity guides acute management, rehabilitation planning, and outcome expectations.

Primary vs. Secondary Brain Injury

4.3 Concussion/Mild TBI Management

Current evidence supports graduated return-to-activity protocols rather than complete cognitive and physical rest beyond the initial 24-48 hours.

Graduated Return-to-Activity Protocol (Amsterdam Consensus 2023):
  • Stage 1: Initial rest and symptom monitoring ‚Äî 24-48 hours of cognitive and physical rest
  • Stage 2: Symptom-limited activity ‚Äî gradual return to daily activities that do not exacerbate symptoms
  • Stage 3: Light aerobic exercise ‚Äî walking, swimming, stationary cycling at <70% max HR
  • Stage 4: Sport-specific exercise ‚Äî running drills, passing, skating drills (no contact/head-impact)
  • Stage 5: Non-contact training drills ‚Äî more complex training, may initiate progressive resistance
  • Stage 6: Full-contact practice ‚Äî after medical clearance and normal neuropsychological testing
  • Stage 7: Return to sport/competitive activity ‚Äî unrestricted participation

Minimum 24 hours at each stage before progressing. Return to previous stage if symptoms recur.

Amsterdam 2023 Updates: Extended protocol from 6 to 7 stages, emphasized initial rest period, incorporated neuropsychological testing requirements, and refined return-to-contact criteria.
Post-Concussion Red Flags (Seek Immediate Medical Attention):
  • Worsening headache that does not resolve
  • Repeated vomiting
  • Seizures
  • Increasing confusion or unusual behavior
  • Weakness or numbness in extremities
  • Slurred speech or inability to recognize people

5.1 Cognitive Rehabilitation Approaches

Cognitive deficits after TBI often have greater impact on functional outcomes and return to work than physical impairments. A systematic approach addressing specific cognitive domains is essential.

Cognitive Domains and Interventions:
  • Attention: Attention Process Training (APT), sustained attention tasks, dual-task training with progressive complexity
  • Memory: External aids (smartphones, calendars, checklists), errorless learning techniques, spaced retrieval, visual imagery mnemonics
  • Executive Function: Goal Management Training, problem-solving exercises, self-monitoring checklists, metacognitive strategy instruction
  • Processing Speed: Time pressure management, computer-based training, strategy development for complex tasks
  • Awareness/Insight: Video self-confrontation, self-prediction activities, structured feedback from therapist and peers

5.2 Behavioral and Emotional Management

Common Behavioral Changes Post-TBI

  • Agitation and aggression: Most common in Rancho Level IV; may require environmental modification, behavioral protocols, pharmacological management
  • Disinhibition: Impulsive behavior, socially inappropriate comments; frontal lobe injury. Address with structured feedback, role-playing
  • Apathy and initiation deficits: Common with frontal injury; establish structured routines, external cueing systems, motivational strategies
  • Emotional lability (pseudobulbar affect): Involuntary laughing/crying disproportionate to mood; may respond to SSRI or dextromethorphan/quinidine

Depression Post-TBI

Affects approximately 25-50% of TBI survivors. Both neurobiological changes and psychosocial adjustment contribute. Screening with PHQ-9 should be routine. Cognitive behavioral therapy adapted for cognitive deficits shows effectiveness.

5.3 Physical Rehabilitation Strategies for TBI

TBI-Specific Physical Rehabilitation Considerations:
  • Balance and vestibular: Vestibular rehabilitation for post-traumatic vertigo/BPPV; balance training progressing static ‚Üí dynamic ‚Üí dual-task
  • Heterotopic ossification (HO): Abnormal bone formation in periarticular soft tissues; 11-20% of moderate-severe TBI. Maintain ROM, monitor for decreased motion
  • Postural dysfunction: Tone management, positioning programs, serial casting for contracture prevention
  • Fatigue management: Energy conservation, activity pacing, structured rest periods
  • Autonomic dysregulation (paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity): Episodes of tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, posturing. Requires medical management and environmental control

5.4 Family Education and Community Reintegration

Family education is critical for long-term outcomes after TBI. Caregivers experience significant burden and require training in behavioral management, safety precautions, and community resources.

Key Family Education Topics:
  • Understanding the nature of brain injury and expected recovery trajectory
  • Behavioral management strategies for agitation, disinhibition, and emotional changes
  • Safety precautions for cognitive and physical deficits
  • Community resources: Brain Injury Association of America, support groups, respite care
  • Signs of caregiver burnout and when to seek help

6.3 SCI Complications

Autonomic Dysreflexia ‚Äî MEDICAL EMERGENCY (injuries T6 and above):
  • Triggers: Bladder distention (most common), bowel impaction, skin irritation, tight clothing
  • Symptoms: Severe hypertension (systolic >20-40 mmHg above baseline), pounding headache, facial flushing, bradycardia, diaphoresis above injury level
  • Immediate management: Sit patient upright, loosen restrictive clothing, check and empty bladder (most common cause), check for fecal impaction, monitor BP
  • If unresolved: Administer nifedipine or nitropaste; call emergency services if BP remains elevated

Additional SCI Complications

  • Pressure injuries: Leading complication; affect up to 80% of SCI patients. Pressure relief every 15-30 minutes, appropriate cushioning, skin inspection
  • Neurogenic bladder: Intermittent catheterization program, monitoring for UTI
  • Neurogenic bowel: Bowel program with consistent timing, adequate fluid and fiber
  • Deep vein thrombosis: Prophylaxis with compression stockings, anticoagulation in acute phase
  • Respiratory complications: Higher injury = greater impairment. Assisted cough techniques, incentive spirometry
  • Orthostatic hypotension: Gradual position changes, abdominal binder, compression stockings

7.1 Parkinson's Disease Pathophysiology

Parkinson's disease (PD) results from progressive loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, disrupting basal ganglia circuitry. Motor symptoms typically appear after 60-80% of dopaminergic neurons are lost.

Cardinal Motor Features of PD (TRAP):
  • Tremor: Resting tremor (4-6 Hz), "pill-rolling," typically asymmetric onset; decreases with voluntary movement
  • Rigidity: "Cogwheel" rigidity (ratchet-like resistance) or "lead-pipe" (continuous resistance); present throughout ROM
  • Akinesia/Bradykinesia: Slowness of movement, decreased amplitude, difficulty initiating movement; most disabling motor feature
  • Postural Instability: Impaired postural reflexes, retropulsion; typically appears later in disease course; major fall risk factor

Hoehn and Yahr Staging

StageDescriptionFunctional Impact
1Unilateral involvementMinimal functional impairment
2Bilateral involvement without balance impairmentDifficulty with dual tasks
3Bilateral with postural instabilityPhysically independent but fall risk
4Severe disability, able to walk/stand unassistedRequires assistance for ADLs
5Wheelchair-bound or bedriddenDependent for care

7.2 Evidence-Based Exercise for Parkinson's Disease

Exercise is considered a disease-modifying intervention in PD with neuroprotective effects. Current evidence strongly supports high-intensity, sustained exercise programs.

LSVT BIG¬Æ Protocol:
  • Amplitude-based treatment addressing bradykinesia/hypokinesia
  • Focus on "thinking BIG" ‚Äî exaggerated, large-amplitude movements
  • Protocol: 4 days/week √ó 4 weeks, 1-hour sessions
  • Strong evidence for improved movement speed, balance, and functional mobility
  • Principles can be incorporated into all therapy activities

Exercise Recommendations

  • Aerobic exercise: 30-40 minutes, 3x/week at 60-80% max HR; treadmill, cycling, swimming. Evidence shows slowed motor decline
  • Resistance training: 2-3x/week targeting all major muscle groups; eccentric emphasis for rigidity
  • Balance training: Tai chi shows strong evidence for reducing falls in PD; also yoga, dance (especially tango)
  • Dual-task training: Combining motor and cognitive tasks to address PD-specific dual-task interference
  • Boxing/boxing-style fitness: High-intensity, engaging program; preliminary evidence for balance and quality of life improvement

7.3 Gait and Freezing Management

Strategies for Freezing of Gait (FOG):
  • External cueing: Rhythmic auditory cueing (metronome), visual cues (laser pointer, floor markers), verbal cues
  • Attentional strategies: "Think about taking big steps," count steps, focus on heel strike
  • Weight shifting: Lateral weight shift before stepping; marching in place
  • Turning strategies: Wide arc turns, avoid pivoting; "clock turn" strategy
  • Avoid triggers: Narrow spaces, crowds, time pressure, dual-task situations during freezing episodes

Fall Prevention in PD

Falls occur in 60-70% of PD patients annually, with recurrent falls in 50%. Multifactorial falls risk assessment and targeted interventions are essential:

  • Home safety assessment and modification
  • Medication review (dopaminergic medications and orthostatic hypotension)
  • Progressive balance and strength training
  • Assistive device training (wheeled walkers preferred over standard walkers)
  • Education on freezing management and safe turning

8.1 MS Pathophysiology and Disease Courses

Multiple sclerosis is a chronic autoimmune demyelinating disease of the CNS affecting approximately 1 million Americans. Understanding disease courses is critical for rehabilitation planning and prognosis.

MS Disease Classifications:
  • Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS) ‚Äî 85%: Clearly defined relapses with full or partial recovery; stable between episodes
  • Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS): Develops from RRMS; gradual worsening with or without relapses
  • Primary Progressive MS (PPMS) ‚Äî 10-15%: Gradual worsening from onset without distinct relapses
  • Progressive-Relapsing MS (PRMS): Rare; progressive from onset with superimposed acute relapses

8.2 Exercise and Rehabilitation in MS

Exercise was historically cautioned against in MS due to concerns about symptom exacerbation. Current strong evidence supports regular exercise as safe and beneficial for fitness, fatigue, mood, and quality of life.

Exercise Guidelines for MS (ACSM/MS Society):
  • Aerobic: 2-3 days/week, 10-30 minutes, moderate intensity (40-70% VO2peak). Progress gradually. Swimming or cycling preferred for heat sensitivity
  • Resistance: 2-3 days/week, 1-2 sets of 8-15 reps, moderate intensity. Major muscle groups, circuit format tolerated well
  • Flexibility: Daily stretching, focus on spastic muscle groups (hip adductors, plantarflexors, hamstrings)
  • Balance: Incorporate into all sessions; progress from static to dynamic to reactive

8.3 Fatigue Management

Fatigue is the most common MS symptom (80% of patients) and the leading cause of disability-related work cessation. It differs from normal fatigue in severity and pattern.

Fatigue Management Strategies:
  • Energy conservation: Activity analysis, work simplification, planning and prioritizing
  • Cooling strategies: Uhthoff's phenomenon ‚Äî heat worsens symptoms. Cooling vests, cold beverages, cooled environments
  • Sleep hygiene: Address sleep disorders (25-54% prevalence in MS); consistent sleep/wake schedule
  • Exercise: Paradoxically, regular moderate exercise reduces fatigue levels over time
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy: Evidence supports CBT for managing MS fatigue
  • Medication: Amantadine, modafinil (limited evidence)

8.4 Heat Sensitivity and Exercise Precautions

Uhthoff's Phenomenon ‚Äî Critical Consideration:

Elevated body temperature (even 0.5¬∞C increase) can temporarily worsen MS symptoms due to impaired nerve conduction in demyelinated fibers. This is NOT a relapse ‚Äî symptoms resolve with cooling.

  • Schedule exercise during cooler times of day
  • Use pre-cooling strategies (cooling vest, cold drinks)
  • Exercise in air-conditioned environments
  • Aquatic therapy in pools 80-84¬∞F (not warmer)
  • Monitor symptoms; stop if significant worsening occurs

9.2 Common Peripheral Neuropathies

Diabetic Peripheral Neuropathy

The most common peripheral neuropathy, affecting 50% of diabetics. Presents as symmetric distal "stocking-glove" pattern sensory loss, progressing proximally.

Rehabilitation for Diabetic Neuropathy:
  • Balance training: Essential ‚Äî loss of proprioception increases fall risk 15-fold
  • Foot care education: Daily inspection, proper footwear, avoid walking barefoot
  • Aerobic exercise: Improves nerve function; moderate intensity cycling/swimming preferred (less foot stress)
  • Pain management: Desensitization, TENS, pharmacological (gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine)
  • Assistive devices: AFOs for foot drop, sensory substitution strategies

Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome (GBS)

Acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy. Ascending weakness peaking at 2-4 weeks. 85% achieve independent ambulation within 6 months with rehabilitation.

  • Acute phase: Respiratory monitoring (25-30% require ventilation), DVT prophylaxis, positioning, gentle PROM
  • Recovery phase: Gradual progressive strengthening (avoid overwork weakness), endurance training, functional mobility
  • Fatigue management: Major residual complaint even after motor recovery; pacing, energy conservation

9.4 Nerve Regeneration and Rehabilitation Principles

Rehabilitation During Nerve Regeneration:
  • Maintain joint mobility: PROM to prevent contractures while awaiting reinnervation
  • Sensory re-education: Begin when protective sensation returns; progress from localization to discrimination
  • Motor re-education: Initiate when EMG shows reinnervation; biofeedback, functional electrical stimulation
  • Splinting: Prevent deformity during denervation period; dynamic splinting as recovery begins
  • Patient education: Protection of denervated skin from injury (burns, pressure)

10.1 Neurological Balance Control Systems

Balance requires integration of visual, vestibular, and somatosensory inputs by the CNS to generate appropriate motor responses. Neurological conditions frequently disrupt one or more of these systems.

Sensory Systems for Balance:
  • Somatosensory (70% on firm surface): Proprioceptors in joints, muscles, skin provide position and movement information
  • Visual (10%): Provides spatial orientation reference; can compensate for vestibular/somatosensory deficits
  • Vestibular (20%): Detects head position/movement via semicircular canals (angular acceleration) and otolith organs (linear acceleration, gravity)

10.2 Vestibular Rehabilitation

Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

The most common vestibular disorder; displaced otoconia in semicircular canals. Posterior canal affected in 85-95% of cases.

Canalith Repositioning Maneuvers:
  • Epley Maneuver: Gold standard for posterior canal BPPV; 70-90% effective in 1-3 treatments
  • Semont Maneuver: Alternative for posterior canal BPPV
  • BBQ Roll (Lempert): For horizontal canal BPPV
  • Diagnosis: Dix-Hallpike test for posterior canal; Roll test for horizontal canal

Vestibular Hypofunction Rehabilitation

Unilateral or bilateral vestibular loss from vestibular neuritis, labyrinthitis, acoustic neuroma, ototoxicity, or surgical ablation.

  • Gaze stabilization exercises: VOR √ó1 and √ó2 viewing ‚Äî head movement while maintaining focus on stationary/moving targets
  • Habituation exercises: Repeated exposure to positions/movements that provoke symptoms to promote central compensation
  • Balance retraining: Progressive challenges to standing balance with altered sensory conditions (foam, eyes closed, head turns)
  • Substitution strategies: For bilateral vestibular loss ‚Äî enhanced use of visual and somatosensory cues

10.3 Cerebellar Disorders

Cerebellar dysfunction produces ataxia ‚Äî incoordination affecting gait, limbs, speech, and eye movements without weakness or sensory loss.

Cerebellar Signs and Rehabilitation Approaches:
  • Dysmetria: Inaccurate reaching; use weighted utensils, stabilization, target practice
  • Intention tremor: Worsens approaching target; proximal stabilization, weighted wrist cuffs
  • Dysdiadochokinesia: Impaired rapid alternating movements; rhythmic training, metronome-paced exercises
  • Ataxic gait (wide-based, irregular): Balance training, assistive devices, visual cueing, gait training with weighted vest
  • Truncal ataxia: Core stabilization, Swiss ball exercises, seated balance progression

10.4 Balance Assessment and Intervention Progression

Neurological Balance Training Progression:
  • Level 1: Static balance ‚Äî firm surface, eyes open ‚Üí eyes closed ‚Üí head turns
  • Level 2: Static balance ‚Äî foam/compliant surface, eyes open ‚Üí eyes closed
  • Level 3: Dynamic balance ‚Äî weight shifting, reaching, perturbation responses
  • Level 4: Gait training ‚Äî over-ground ‚Üí obstacles ‚Üí varied surfaces ‚Üí dual-task
  • Level 5: Advanced ‚Äî community mobility, stairs, uneven terrain, time-pressured tasks

11.1 Cerebral Palsy

Cerebral palsy is the most common motor disability in childhood, affecting 1 in 345 children. It results from non-progressive damage to the developing brain, though functional manifestations change over time.

CP Classification:
  • Spastic CP (70-80%): UMN pattern ‚Äî increased tone, hyperreflexia. Subtypes: hemiplegia, diplegia, quadriplegia
  • Dyskinetic CP (10-15%): Basal ganglia involvement ‚Äî athetosis (slow writhing), dystonia (sustained postures), chorea (irregular jerky movements)
  • Ataxic CP (5-10%): Cerebellar involvement ‚Äî hypotonia, coordination deficits, intention tremor
  • Mixed CP: Combination of types; most commonly spastic + dyskinetic

Gross Motor Function Classification System (GMFCS)

LevelDescription (ages 6-12)
IWalks without limitations; limited advanced motor skills
IIWalks with limitations; difficulty with uneven surfaces, stairs without railing
IIIWalks using hand-held mobility device indoors; wheelchair for community
IVSelf-mobility with limitations; transported or power wheelchair in community
VTransported in wheelchair in all settings; limited head/trunk control

Evidence-Based Interventions for CP

  • Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy (pediatric): Strong evidence for improving UE function in hemiplegic CP
  • Bimanual Intensive Training (HABIT): Equivalent outcomes to CIMT with better bimanual coordination
  • Goal-directed/activity-focused therapy: Task-specific training superior to passive approaches
  • Hippotherapy: Horse-riding therapy with moderate evidence for gross motor function and postural control
  • Botulinum toxin + therapy: Focal spasticity management; must combine with active rehabilitation

11.2 Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

ALS is a progressive neurodegenerative disease affecting both upper and lower motor neurons. Median survival is 3-5 years from symptom onset. Rehabilitation focus shifts from restorative to compensatory to palliative.

Rehabilitation Across ALS Stages:
  • Early stage: Maintain function with moderate-intensity exercise (avoid high resistance); ROM, aerobic fitness, energy conservation education
  • Middle stage: Compensatory strategies, assistive devices, home modifications, respiratory management (cough assist, non-invasive ventilation)
  • Late stage: Comfort care, positioning, communication devices (eye-tracking technology), caregiver training, palliative care coordination
Exercise Precautions in ALS:
  • Avoid eccentric overload and high-resistance exercise in weakened muscles (risk of overwork weakness)
  • Monitor for signs of respiratory compromise (FVC <50% predicted indicates significant respiratory weakness)
  • Gentle ROM to prevent contractures; avoid aggressive stretching
  • Focus on function preservation rather than strength gains

11.3 Huntington's Disease

Autosomal dominant neurodegenerative disease affecting basal ganglia. Triad of motor, cognitive, and psychiatric symptoms. Average onset age 35-44 years.

  • Motor: Chorea (involuntary dance-like movements), dystonia, gait instability, dysarthria, dysphagia
  • Cognitive: Executive dysfunction, processing speed deficits, progressing to dementia
  • Psychiatric: Depression (39%), irritability, apathy, obsessive-compulsive behaviors
  • Rehabilitation: Maintain mobility and function as long as possible; fall prevention paramount; adaptive equipment for chorea; swallowing safety; caregiver support

12.1 Technology in Neurorehabilitation

Technological advances are transforming neurological rehabilitation, offering new possibilities for intensive, engaging, and measurable interventions.

Emerging Technologies:
  • Robotic-Assisted Therapy: Upper extremity (InMotion, Armeo) and lower extremity (Lokomat, Ekso, ReWalk). Provides consistent, high-repetition practice; best as supplement to conventional therapy
  • Virtual Reality: Immersive (head-mounted) and non-immersive (screen-based). Evidence supports improved UE function post-stroke, balance in PD, gait in MS
  • Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI): Direct neural control of devices; emerging evidence for motor recovery through neurofeedback
  • Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS): Non-invasive brain stimulation; growing evidence as adjunct to motor rehabilitation
  • Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS): Low-cost neuromodulation; mixed but promising evidence for motor learning enhancement
  • Wearable Sensors and Telerehabilitation: Remote monitoring of movement quality, adherence, and function; enables home-based intensive programs

12.3 Discharge Planning and Community Reintegration

Successful transition from rehabilitation to community requires comprehensive discharge planning addressing physical, cognitive, psychosocial, and environmental factors.

Comprehensive Discharge Planning Checklist:
  • Home assessment: Accessibility, safety modifications, equipment placement
  • Equipment needs: DME ordered and fitted (wheelchair, hospital bed, bath equipment, adaptive devices)
  • Caregiver training: Transfers, mobility assistance, skin care, medication management, emergency procedures
  • Outpatient therapy: Scheduled follow-up, home exercise program provided and demonstrated
  • Community resources: Support groups, day programs, vocational rehabilitation, transportation services
  • Driving assessment: Referral for driving evaluation when appropriate; discuss restrictions
  • Return to work/school: Functional capacity evaluation, workplace accommodations, gradual return plan

12.4 Outcome Measurement and Evidence-Based Practice

Key Neurological Rehabilitation Outcome Measures:
  • FIM/WeeFIM: Functional independence across motor and cognitive domains
  • Barthel Index: ADL independence; widely used internationally
  • Modified Rankin Scale: Global disability after stroke (0-6)
  • 10-Meter Walk Test: Gait speed; <0.8 m/s indicates limited community ambulation
  • 6-Minute Walk Test: Walking endurance; community ambulation requires ~300m
  • Timed Up and Go (TUG): Functional mobility; >13.5 seconds indicates fall risk
  • Patient-Reported Outcomes: Quality of life (SF-36, EQ-5D), depression screening (PHQ-9)
Evidence-Based Practice Reminder:

Neurological rehabilitation should integrate the best available research evidence with clinical expertise and patient values/preferences. Stay current with clinical practice guidelines from the American Heart Association, APTA Neurology Section, and relevant specialty organizations.
