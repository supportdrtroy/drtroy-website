Module 1: Fall Prevention Fundamentals

Shared Content - 45 minutes

1.1 Fall Statistics and Impact

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death among adults 65 and older, with one in four older adults falling each year. Understanding the scope and impact of falls is essential for all healthcare providers.

Key Statistics

  • Falls result in 32,000 deaths per year in the United States
  • Medical costs for falls exceed $50 billion annually
  • 20% of falls result in serious injury requiring medical treatment
  • Fear of falling affects 20-60% of older adults, leading to activity restriction

1.2 Multifactorial Fall Risk Model

Falls result from complex interactions between intrinsic (person-related) and extrinsic (environmental) factors.

Clinical Pearl: The risk of falling increases exponentially with the number of risk factors present. Individuals with 4 or more risk factors have a 78% probability of falling within the next year.

1.3 Evidence-Based Fall Prevention

Research has identified several interventions with strong evidence for fall prevention effectiveness:

Level A Evidence (Strong Research Support)

  • Exercise Programs: Tai Chi, balance training, strengthening
  • Medication Review: Reducing psychoactive medications
  • Vision Assessment: Cataract surgery, prescription updates
  • Home Safety Assessment: Environmental modification

Level B Evidence (Moderate Research Support)

  • Vitamin D Supplementation: For deficient individuals
  • Footwear Assessment: Appropriate, well-fitting shoes
  • Cognitive Behavioral Interventions: Addressing fear of falling

2A.1 Comprehensive Fall Risk Assessment (PT)

As the evaluating clinician, PTs must conduct systematic assessments to identify fall risk factors and develop evidence-based intervention plans.

Clinical Decision Making

PTs must synthesize assessment findings to determine:

  • Primary impairments: Strength, balance, coordination, range of motion
  • Activity limitations: Functional mobility restrictions
  • Participation restrictions: Impact on life roles and activities
  • Prognosis: Expected outcomes and timeframe
  • Plan of care: Interventions, goals, frequency, duration

PT Case Study: Complex Fall Risk Assessment

Patient: 72-year-old female with history of stroke (6 months ago), diabetes, and recent fall at home.

Assessment Findings:

  • Berg Balance Scale: 38/56 (high fall risk)
  • Timed Up and Go: 18 seconds with walker
  • Left hemiparesis with 3+/5 strength lower extremity
  • Decreased sensation left foot
  • Takes 8 medications including sedating antihistamine

PT Clinical Reasoning: What are your primary concerns and intervention priorities? How would you develop a comprehensive plan of care?

PTAs assist with data collection and implement standardized assessments as directed by the supervising PT.

PTA Role in Assessment

  • Data Collection: Perform specific tests as directed by PT
  • Observation: Monitor patient performance and safety
  • Documentation: Record objective findings accurately
  • Communication: Report significant changes to supervising PT

Recognizing Red Flags

PTAs must recognize when to stop assessment and consult the supervising PT:

  • Safety concerns: Unsteadiness, near falls, patient fear
  • Vital sign changes: Significant increase in HR, BP, shortness of breath
  • Pain: New or worsening pain during mobility
  • Cognitive changes: Confusion, disorientation
  • Patient decline: Performance worse than expected

PTA Case Study: Mobility Assessment Implementation

Scenario: You're working with a 68-year-old male post-knee replacement (2 weeks) who was referred for fall prevention following a fall at home.

PT Plan: "Please perform TUG test, observe gait with walker, and note any balance concerns during transfers."

Your Observations:

  • TUG: 22 seconds with front-wheeled walker
  • Gait: Short steps, wide base, excessive reliance on walker
  • Transfers: Requires minimal assist sit-to-stand
  • Patient reports knee pain 4/10 during activity

PTA Decision Making: How do you document these findings? What would you communicate to the supervising PT?

Module 3: Transfer and Mobility Techniques

Shared Content with Role-Specific Applications - 60 minutes

3.1 Safe Transfer Principles

Effective transfers require proper body mechanics, environmental setup, and patient preparation. Both PTs and PTAs must demonstrate competency in transfer techniques.

Universal Transfer Safety Rules

  1. Plan the transfer: Clear path, stable surfaces, appropriate equipment
  2. Communicate: Explain the process, use clear verbal cues
  3. Position yourself: Wide base of support, bend knees, keep back straight
  4. Use patient's strength: Encourage maximum independence
  5. Maintain safety: Be prepared to assist or abort if unsafe

PT ADVANCED TECHNIQUES

PTs determine transfer methods, train other staff, and handle complex transfer situations.

3.3 PT Role: Transfer Assessment and Training

Complex Transfer Situations

  • Neurological impairments: Hemiplegia, Parkinson's, SCI
  • Cognitive impairments: Dementia, delirium, confusion
  • Multiple comorbidities: Obesity, CHF, COPD
  • Post-surgical: Weight-bearing restrictions, precautions
  • Equipment dependencies: Lines, tubes, monitors

Teaching Transfer Techniques

PTs are responsible for:

  • Training PTAs in appropriate transfer methods
  • Educating family members and caregivers
  • Determining when transfers are contraindicated
  • Modifying techniques based on patient progress
  • Determining equipment needs and training others in use
PTA IMPLEMENTATION

PTAs execute transfer plans, provide skilled assistance, and monitor patient progress.

3.3 PTA Role: Transfer Implementation and Progression

Skilled Transfer Interventions

  • Hands-on assistance: Providing appropriate level of support
  • Cueing and coaching: Verbal and tactile cues for safety
  • Equipment setup: Proper positioning and use of devices
  • Environmental management: Ensuring safe transfer space
  • Progress monitoring: Advancing or regressing assistance level

When to Modify or Stop

PTAs must recognize when to:

  • Increase assistance: Patient showing fatigue, unsteadiness, fear
  • Use different technique: Patient not responding to current method
  • Seek PT consultation: Unexpected difficulties or regression
  • Stop activity: Safety concerns or medical changes
PTA Clinical Pearl: Always err on the side of safety. It's better to provide more assistance than needed than to risk a fall or injury.
